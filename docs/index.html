SynthBench measures how well synthetic respondent systems reproduce real human survey response distributions. Scores are computed against ground-truth data from OpinionsQA (Santurkar et al., ICML 2023) — 1,498 questions from the Pew American Trends Panel.

Composite Parity combines distributional similarity (Jensen-Shannon Divergence) and rank-order agreement (Kendall's tau) into a single 0–1 score. Higher is better.

Metric Explanations

Composite Parity combines distributional similarity and rank-order agreement into a single 0–1 score. (1 - JSD + (1 + tau)/2) / 2. Higher is better.

P_dist (Distributional Parity) = 1 - mean(JSD). Measures how closely a provider's response distribution matches the human distribution. Jensen-Shannon divergence is bounded [0, 1], symmetric, and handles zero entries. A perfect match yields P_dist = 1.

P_rank (Rank-Order Parity) = (1 + mean(tau)) / 2. Kendall's tau-b on probability rankings, normalized to [0, 1]. Captures whether the provider gets the ordering of options right, even when exact proportions differ.

P_refuse (Refusal Calibration) = 1 - mean(|R_provider - R_human|). Whether the provider's refusal rate matches human refusal patterns. Human refusal patterns carry signal: questions about religion see higher refusals from non-religious respondents; income questions see higher refusals from high earners.

Mean JSD: Average Jensen-Shannon divergence across all questions. Lower is better (0 = identical distributions).

Kendall's tau: Rank correlation between human and model response option rankings. Range [-1, 1]. 1 = perfect agreement, 0 = no correlation.

Baselines give meaning to scores:

Random Baseline: Uniform distribution over all options. The absolute floor — any provider scoring at or below random is adding negative value.

Majority-Class Baseline: Always picks the mode of the population distribution. Scores well on consensus questions, poorly on divisive ones. Shows the value of distributional modeling.
