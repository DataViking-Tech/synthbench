SynthBench

Open benchmark for synthetic survey respondent quality

GitHub Methodology Submit Results

SynthBench measures how well synthetic respondent systems reproduce real human survey response distributions. Scores are computed against ground-truth data from OpinionsQA (Santurkar et al., ICML 2023) — 1,498 questions from the Pew American Trends Panel.

SynthBench is maintained by DataViking-Tech, which also develops SynthPanel. All results use identical evaluation methodology.

SPS (SynthBench Parity Score) measures how well AI reproduces human survey responses. Higher is better (0 = random, 1 = human-identical).

P_dist — distributional match (1 − JSD). P_rank — rank-order agreement (normalized Kendall’s τ). P_refuse — refusal-rate calibration (1 − mean |Δrefusal|). All [0, 1]; higher = better.

Understanding the Scores

SPS — SynthBench Parity Score
The overall score — average of all metrics below. 0 = random noise, 1 = indistinguishable from real humans.
Example: An SPS of 0.72 means the AI reproduces about 72% of the fidelity of actual human survey responses.

P_dist — Distributional Parity
How closely does the AI’s answer distribution match real humans? If 60% of humans say “yes” and the AI says “yes” 60% of the time, that’s a perfect match.
0 = completely different distributions. 1 = identical distributions.
Example: On “Do you support renewable energy?” humans split 70/30. If the AI also splits 70/30, P_dist is near 1. If the AI splits 50/50, P_dist drops.

P_rank — Rank-Order Parity
Does the AI get the preference ordering right? If humans prefer A > B > C, does the AI agree — even if the exact percentages differ?
0 = reversed or random ordering. 1 = perfect rank agreement.
Example: Humans rank “economy” > “healthcare” > “education” as top priorities. The AI agrees on that ordering even though its percentages are slightly off — that’s high P_rank.

P_refuse — Refusal Calibration
Does the AI refuse to answer at appropriate rates? Humans sometimes decline sensitive questions. An AI that never refuses, or refuses too often, is miscalibrated.
0 = refusal rates are completely off. 1 = refusal rates match humans exactly.
Example: 15% of humans decline to answer about income. If the AI also declines ~15% of the time, P_refuse is high. If it always answers, P_refuse drops.

P_cond — Conditioning Fidelity
When we tell the AI “respond as a 65-year-old conservative,” does its answer actually shift to match that demographic? Higher means better demographic role-playing.
0 = personas have no effect on answers. 1 = perfect demographic conditioning.
Example: Young liberals and older conservatives answer differently in real life. If the AI shifts its answers appropriately when given each persona, P_cond is high.

P_sub — Subgroup Consistency
Is the AI equally accurate across all demographics, or does it nail some groups and miss others?
0 = wildly uneven accuracy across groups. 1 = equally accurate for every demographic.
Example: An AI that nails young liberals but misrepresents older conservatives gets penalized. Consistent accuracy across all groups earns a high P_sub.

Technical Details

SPS = equal-weighted mean of active component metrics. SPS = mean(P_dist, P_rank, P_refuse[, P_cond, P_sub])

P_dist = 1 - mean(JSD). Jensen-Shannon divergence is bounded [0, 1], symmetric, and handles zero entries.

P_rank = (1 + mean(tau)) / 2. Kendall’s tau-b on probability rankings, normalized to [0, 1].

P_refuse = 1 - mean(|R_provider - R_human|).

P_cond: Improvement from persona conditioning vs. unconditioned baseline.

P_sub: Variance across per-group P_dist scores, inverted so higher is better.

Mean JSD: Average Jensen-Shannon divergence across all questions. Lower is better (0 = identical distributions).

Kendall’s tau: Rank correlation between human and model response option rankings. Range [-1, 1]. 1 = perfect agreement, 0 = no correlation.

Baselines give meaning to scores:

Random Baseline: Uniform distribution over all options. The absolute floor — any provider scoring at or below random is adding negative value.

Majority-Class Baseline: Always picks the mode of the population distribution. Scores well on consensus questions, poorly on divisive ones. Shows the value of distributional modeling.
